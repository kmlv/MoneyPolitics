{% blocktrans trimmed %} General Idea {% endblocktrans %}

{% blocktrans trimmed %} This experiment consists of X rounds of interaction. At the beginning of each round, you will be randomly assigned to a group of nine participants. All rounds will have a similar structure and rules: You will first perform a task. Your initial wealth will be determined by your performance in the task or by luck. Then all the participants will choose a tax level that will apply to the whole group. Before choosing the tax level, you may receive the chance to send a message to other participants. We detail the rules below. {% endblocktrans %}

{% blocktrans trimmed %} Generating Initial Wealth {% endblocktrans %}

In every round, every group member will perform a task. After the task, the computer will generate a ranking of performance in the task for all nine participants. Your position in the ranking might or might not determine your initial wealth following a probabilistic rule.

There will be six wealth levels: 9, 15, 25, 40, 80, and 125. If your wealth is generated by your ranking the following table will apply:

Income Assignment by Skill 125 80 40 25 25 15 15 15 9
Ranking in Real Effort Task 1 2 3 4 5 6 7 8 9

Notice there are nine participants, but only six different wealth levels. Two participants, ranks 4 and 5, both have wealth level 25, and three participants, ranks 6, 7 and 8, each have wealth level 15.

Your wealth might be determined by performance in the task or by luck, as follows. After rankings are determined, the computer will flip a virtual coin. If it comes up heads, six participants’ will have their initial wealth be determined by their ranking in the task, and the wealth of the other three will be randomly determined by shuffling them from the positions they would have occupied according to the table. If it comes up tails, only three participants’ initial wealth will be determined by their ranking in the task, and the wealth of each of the other six will be determined by random shuffling. That is, with a 50% chance, the majority of the nine participants will have their initial wealth be determined by their performance, and with the remaining 50% chance, the majority of participants’ wealth will be determined by luck. Those whose initial wealth is not determined by their performance are assigned the wealth associated with another member’s performance, by way of random position shuffling.

{% blocktrans trimmed %} Selecting a Tax Rate for the Group {% endblocktrans %}

Participants’ initial wealth levels help to determine their earnings in the experiment by serving as a potential resource that they may invest in an economic activity. Exactly how much each member earns is also determined by (1) the amounts of public goods (analogous to roads, bridges, public transit, and other government services in the real world) that are funded by taxes paid by the participants, and (2) the amount of tax that each individual pays. In general, you’ll earn more if there are more public goods being funded by the tax payments of the group as a whole, and you’ll earn more if the amount of tax that you personally pay is lower, since paying more tax leaves you less to put into your own economic activity.

Once all participants have been informed of their initial wealth levels, the group will collectively decide the tax level that will be binding for all participants. Each person will vote for a tax rate that they would like to be applied to all participants, themselves included. Once the votes are submitted, the median of all submitted preferred tax rates will be determined and selected as the tax rate applicable to the group.

How is the median determined?

In the context of nine values, the median M is defined as the value such that there are five numbers that are smaller than or equal to M; and there are five numbers that are greater than or equal to M. For example, suppose you and your friends must all choose an amount to contribute to pay for the food at a party. You select $5, and the other eight participants of your group have chosen: $1, $4, $8, $9, $10, $7, $8, $2. To find the median number, you can sort from low to high as in the table below, then find the number in the middle position (position 5). That is the median.

Number Order 1 2 3 4 5 6 7 8 9
Contribution $2 $3 $3 $6 $6 $9 $10 $12 $16

The median in the example would be $6, which is the middle amount in a sorted list. When you and other participants select a tax rate, the median amount will be computed in this fashion.

{% blocktrans trimmed %} Payoffs {% endblocktrans %}

The payoff for the round is calculated as follows. First, the total cost of the messages you sent is subtracted from initial wealth. Then each member's initial wealth is taxed at the chosen (median) tax rate. This generates two relevant values: what is left after taxes (a private resource) and the total amount of tax revenue (viewed as resources that fund public goods).

The payoff takes into consideration the resources that have been paid to the government. In particular, we consider the direct benefit from public goods (ex.: the benefit of social security, or unemployment insurance) and the indirect benefit via positive effects of public goods on our own private productivity (ex.: the benefit that companies receive from having roads and private property rights).
